# CAOS AI Policy

---

## Purpose

AI tools are becoming embedded in nearly every aspect of how organizations work. This policy defines how CAOS uses AI in its own operations, what values guide those choices, and where we stand publicly on AI in assistive technology. It is not a technical manual — it is a statement of priorities and accountability.

---

## Scope

This policy applies to:

- All staff, volunteers, and contractors acting on behalf of CAOS
- AI tools used in CAOS governance, operations, communications, and programs
- CAOS public positions and advocacy related to AI and assistive technology

---

## Policy Statement

### AI is a tool, not a default

We use AI when it makes sense. Before reaching for an AI tool, we ask whether it is actually the right tool for the job. AI offers real efficiency and quality benefits in the right contexts — drafting, summarizing, accessibility checking, research assistance — but it also carries costs: environmental, economic, and in terms of accuracy and accountability. We apply it deliberately, not reflexively.

### We choose AI tools with intention

When we do use AI, we choose tools that align with our values. We prioritize models and platforms that:

- Demonstrate meaningful understanding of disability and accessibility
- Are trained on or informed by accessibility best practices
- Reflect open-source values where feasible
- Are transparent about their training data, limitations, and governance

We do not treat all AI tools as equivalent. A tool's accessibility awareness and ethical posture are part of our evaluation criteria, not afterthoughts.

### We take environmental and economic impacts seriously

AI model inference and training carry real environmental costs — energy consumption, water use, carbon footprint. We commit to:

- Selecting models appropriate in scale to the task (not using large models where small ones suffice)
- Factoring environmental impact into procurement decisions
- Limiting unnecessary or casual AI use that generates costs without commensurate benefit

We are a small organization and our individual impact is modest, but our choices reflect our values and set an example for the communities we serve.

### We support open-source AI with accessibility awareness

CAOS actively supports the development and promotion of open-source AI models that include nuanced, accurate, and respectful understanding of disability and accessibility. The mainstream AI ecosystem underrepresents disabled perspectives in training data, evaluation, and design. We believe open-source development, done well, offers a path to more accountable and inclusive AI. Specific activities in support of this commitment will be developed as part of CAOS's strategic planning process.

### Our public position on AI in assistive technology

AI has genuine potential to expand what assistive technology can do for people with disabilities. We are not skeptical of that potential — we are insistent that it be realized responsibly.

CAOS publicly advocates for the following principles in AI-powered assistive technology:

**Reliability.** AT users depend on their tools. AI components in AT must meet higher reliability standards than general-purpose consumer AI. Failures in AT are not inconveniences — they can be barriers to communication, safety, employment, and independence.

**Quality.** High accuracy matters more in AT than in many other domains. An AI that gets it wrong 10% of the time may be fine for casual use; in AT, that error rate can be disqualifying.

**User control.** Disabled people must be able to understand, adjust, and override AI behavior in their AT. Black-box AI that cannot be configured or corrected is not acceptable in tools on which people depend for access.

**Privacy.** AT often processes deeply personal information — communications, health data, location, behavioral patterns. AI components in AT must meet rigorous privacy standards. Data collected to improve AI models must be handled with explicit, informed user consent.

**Appropriateness.** Not every AT problem benefits from an AI solution. Vendors should be honest about when AI adds value and when it adds complexity, cost, or risk without commensurate benefit.

We will express this position in public statements, partnerships, vendor engagement, and participation in relevant standards and policy processes.

## Responsibilities

### Board of Directors

- Adopt and maintain this policy
- Ensure CAOS's public AI advocacy positions are consistent with organizational values and bylaws
- Authorize formal public statements on AI and assistive technology

### Executive Director / Board President

- Oversee responsible AI use across CAOS operations
- Represent CAOS's AI policy positions in external engagements
- Ensure staff and volunteers are aware of and follow this policy
- Maintain and update the Board-approved list of AI tools, models, and resources approved for CAOS use
- Bring requests for additions or changes to the approved list to the Board for review

### Staff and Volunteers

- Use only AI tools, models, and resources on the current Board-approved list for CAOS work
- Submit requests for additions or changes to the approved list through established CAOS governance channels
- Flag AI uses that may conflict with this policy
- Do not use AI tools that process sensitive CAOS or community member data without explicit authorization

### Everyone

When in doubt about whether a particular AI use is appropriate under this policy, ask before proceeding.

## Procedures

### Approved AI tools list

The Board maintains a list of AI tools, models, and resources approved for use in CAOS work. Staff and volunteers should consult the current list before adopting any AI tool for CAOS purposes.

To request that a tool be added to or removed from the approved list, contributors may use established CAOS governance channels — opening a GitHub issue in the governance repository is the standard mechanism. Requests should address:

1. **Is AI the right tool?** What is the alternative, and why is AI better for this task?
2. **Accessibility and disability awareness:** Does the tool demonstrate meaningful understanding of accessibility? Has it been evaluated by or with disabled users?
3. **Open-source alignment:** Is the tool open-source or built on open-source models? If not, does the vendor's posture otherwise align with our values?
4. **Environmental impact:** Is the model appropriate in scale? Does the vendor publish or commit to sustainability practices?
5. **Data handling:** What data does the tool collect or process? Is it consistent with CAOS's privacy commitments and our community's expectations?
6. **Cost:** Is the economic cost proportionate to the benefit?

The Board reviews requests and updates the approved list as needed.

### Public advocacy on AI in assistive technology

When CAOS publishes a statement, signs onto a coalition position, or engages a vendor or standards body on AI in AT, that engagement should be:

- Consistent with the principles in this policy
- Authorized by the Executive Director or Board President for routine engagement, or the Board for major formal positions
- Documented in the organizational record

CAOS's AI and AT advocacy position is currently a statement of organizational philosophy. Specific programs, partnerships, and structured advocacy activities will be developed through the CAOS strategic planning process.

### Staying current

AI is moving fast. This policy will be reviewed annually rather than on the standard three-year cycle, given how rapidly the landscape is changing.
